1. Introduction
Welcome
We hope that your employment with Baal Perazim Wellness & Health, PC (INC) (d/b/a Wellness Home and Wellness Home Behavioral Health) (hereafter referred to as the “Practice”) will be rewarding and challenging.

We take pride in our employees as well as in the products and services we provide. You have joined a diverse, inclusive organization dedicated to providing health and wellness services to our patients. We are committed to delivering health and wellness services to everyone regardless of socio-economic, geographic, cultural, or demographic factors.

The Practice complies with all local, state and federal employment laws. The employment policies and/or benefits summaries in this handbook are written for all employees.

This handbook supersedes any previously issued handbooks or policy statements dealing with the subjects discussed herein. The Practice reserves the right to interpret, modify, or discontinue policies contained in this handbook, at any time without prior notice. Neither this handbook nor any other communication by a management representative, whether oral or written, is intended in any way to create a contract of employment. While no employee handbook can address every situation in the workplace, this handbook outlines the Practice’s employment policies and procedures and sets expectations regarding employee behaviors and actions in the workplace.

Please take the time to read this handbook carefully. It is each employee’s responsibility to review and comply with all Practice policies and procedures. As an employee of the Practice, every employee must sign the acknowledgment at the end to confirm that they have read, understand, and agree to the contents of this handbook, which sets out the basic rules and guidelines concerning their employment.

If an employee has questions about their employment or any provisions in this handbook, please contact the People Operations Department.

We wish all employees success in their employment with the Practice!

2. Employment Policies
At-Will Employment
Employment with the Practice is on an “at-will” basis which means an employee may be terminated at any time, with or without notice and with or without cause. Likewise, we respect an employee’s right to resign their employment with the Practice at any time, with or without cause.

Nothing in this handbook or any other Practice document should be understood as creating a contract, guaranteed or continued employment, a right to termination only “for cause,” or any other guarantee of continued benefits or employment. Only the Chief Wellness Officer (CWO) has the authority to make promises or negotiate with regard to guaranteed or continued employment, and any such promises are only effective if placed in writing and signed by the CWO. If a written contract between an employee and the Practice is inconsistent with this handbook, the written contract is controlling.

Nothing in this handbook will be interpreted, applied, or enforced to interfere with, restrain, or coerce employees in the exercise of their rights under Section 7 of the National Labor Relations Act.
Revisions to Handbook
This handbook is intended attempt to keep employees informed of the terms and conditions of employment, including Practice policies and procedures. The handbook is not a contract. The Practice reserves the right to revise, add, or delete from this handbook as we determine to be in our best interest with or without notice, except the policy concerning at-will employment. When changes are made to the policies and guidelines contained herein, the Practice will endeavor to communicate them in a timely fashion, typically in a written supplement to the handbook or in a posting on Practice bulletin boards.

Gender Affirming Acceptable Terms
Gender Identity: A person’s internal, deeply-felt sense of being male, female, or something other or in-between, regardless of the sex they were assigned at birth. Everyone has a gender identity. Gender expression: An individual’s characteristics and behaviors (such as appearance, dress, mannerisms, speech patterns, and social interactions) that may be perceived as masculine or feminine.

Transgender: An umbrella term that can be used to describe people whose gender identity and/or expression is different from their sex assigned at birth. A person whose sex assigned at birth was female but who identifies as male is a transgender man (also known as female-to-male transgender person, or FTM). A person whose sex assigned at birth was male but who identifies as female is a transgender woman (also known as male-to-female transgender person, or MTF). Some people described by this definition do not consider themselves transgender. They may use other words, or may identify simply as a man or woman. A person does not need to identify as transgender in order for the Practice’s nondiscrimination policies to apply to them.

Gender Non-Conforming: This term describes people who have, or are perceived to have, gender characteristics and/or behaviors that do not conform to traditional or societal expectations. It is important to keep in mind that these expectations can vary across cultures and may change over time.

Transition: The process of changing one’s gender from the sex assigned at birth to one’s gender identity. There are many different ways to transition. For some people, it is a complex process that takes place over a long period of time, while for others it is a one- or two-step process that happens more quickly. Transition may include “coming out” (telling family, friends, and coworkers); changing a name and/or sex on legal documents; and, for many transgender people, accessing medical treatment such as hormones and surgery.

Sexual Orientation: A person’s physical or emotional attraction to people of the same and/or other gender. Straight, gay, and bisexual are some ways to describe sexual orientation. It is important to note that sexual orientation is distinct from gender identity and expression. Transgender people can be gay, lesbian, bisexual, or straight, just like non-transgender people.

LGBTQ: A common abbreviation that refers to the lesbian, gay, bisexual, and transgender community.

Commitment to Transgender Employees
Name and Pronouns
An employee has the right to be addressed by the name and pronoun that correspond to the employee’s gender identity, upon request. A court-ordered name or gender change is not required. The intentional or persistent refusal to respect an employee’s gender identity (for example, intentionally referring to the employee by a name or pronoun that does not correspond to the employee’s gender identity) can constitute harassment and is a violation of this policy. In cases where coworkers are unsure of a pronoun preference, the Practice advises employees to politely ask the coworker how they would like to be addressed.

Restroom Accessibility
All of Practice’s restroom facilities that dwell within a Wellness home are gender neutral. In cases where the Practice shares restroom space with other organizations, efforts will be made to advocate making restrooms gender neutral and/or ensuring that employees have the right to use a restroom that corresponds to the employee’s gender identity, regardless of the employee’s sex assigned at birth.

Privacy
Transgender employees have the right to discuss their gender identity or expression openly, or to keep that information private. The transgender employee gets to decide when, with whom, and how much to share their private information. Information about an employee’s transgender status (such as the sex they were assigned at birth) can constitute confidential medical information under privacy laws like HIPAA. Executive Leadership, People Operations, Managers, Supervisors and/or coworkers shall not disclose information that may reveal an employee’s transgender status or gender non-conforming presentation to others. That type of personal or confidential information may only be shared with the transgender employee’s consent and with coworkers who truly need to know to do their jobs.

It is a violation of the Practice’s Diversity, Equity & Inclusion Policy to discriminate in any way (including, but not limited to, failure to hire, failure to promote, or unlawful termination) against an employee because of the employee’s actual or perceived gender identity. Additionally, it is unlawful and contrary to this policy to retaliate against any person objecting to, or supporting enforcement of legal protections against, gender identity discrimination in employment. Our Practice is committed to creating a safe work environment for transgender and gender non-conforming employees. Any incident of discrimination, harassment, or violence based on gender identity or expression will be given immediate and effective attention, including, but not limited to, investigating the incident, taking suitable corrective action, and providing employees and staff with appropriate resources.

Policy Against Workplace Harassment
The Practice has a strict policy against all types of workplace harassment, including sexual harassment and other forms of workplace harassment based upon an individual’s actual or perceived age (40 and older), race, color, national origin, ancestry, religion, sex, pregnancy (including childbirth, lactation, and related medical conditions), marital status, order of protection status, citizenship status, employment status, arrest and conviction information, credit history, crime victim status, physical or mental disability, genetic information (including testing and characteristics), sexual orientation, veteran status, uniformed servicemember status, or any other status protected by federal, state, or local laws. All forms of harassment of, or by, employees, non-employees including contractors and consultants, vendors, visitors, customers, and clients are strictly prohibited and will not be tolerated. If after an investigation, an employee is found to be in violation of this policy the employee will be subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination of employment depending on the circumstances surrounding the incident.
Sexual Harassment
“Sexual harassment” is defined as any (i) unwelcome sexual advances or unwelcome conduct of a sexual nature; (ii) requests for sexual favors or conduct of a sexual nature when (1) submission to such conduct is made either explicitly or implicitly a term or condition of an individual’s employment;, or (2) submission to or rejection of such conduct by an individual is used as the basis for any employment decision affecting the individual;, or (3) such conduct has the purpose or effect of substantially interfering with an individual’s work performance or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment; or (iii) sexual misconduct, which means any behavior of a sexual nature which also involves coercion, abuse of authority, or misuse of an individual’s employment position. An individual’s work environment is not limited to the physical location where an individual is assigned to perform work duties.

Sexual harassment can occur in many ways. It may involve unwelcome romantic or sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, visual materials, social media posts, verbal comments, and/or physical contact of a sexual nature, regardless of gender or gender identity. Involved parties, either victim or harasser, could be a co-worker, subordinate, manager/supervisor, contractor, or even a customer.

Such conduct is a violation of this policy, even in instances where the offending employee believed they were acting jokingly. Such communications, comments, actions of a sexual nature, or unwelcome advances are prohibited at the Practice whether or not other employees were offended.

While it is not possible to identify every act that constitutes or may constitute sexual harassment, the following are some examples of sexual harassment:
  • Unwelcome requests for sexual favors,
  • Lewd or derogatory comments or jokes,
  • Comments regarding sexual behavior or the body of another,
  • Sexual innuendo and other vocal activity such as catcalls or whistles,
  • Obscene letters, notes, emails, invitations, photographs, cartoons, articles, or other written or pictorial materials of a sexual nature,
  • Discussions or inquiries about sexual fantasy, preferences, history, or sex life about self or others,
  • Repeated requests for dates after being informed that interest is unwelcome,
  • Retaliating against another for refusing a sexual advance or reporting an incident of possible sexual harassment to the Practice or any government agency,
  • Offering or providing favors or employment benefits such as promotions, favorable evaluations, favorable assigned duties or shifts, etc., in exchange for sexual favors,
  • Any unwanted physical touching or assaults, or blocking or impeding movements,
  • Other harassment.
Harassment is any unwelcome conduct on the basis of an individual’s actual or perceived age (40 and older), race, color, national origin, ancestry, religion, sex, pregnancy (including childbirth, lactation, and related medical conditions), marital status, order of protection status, citizenship status, arrest and conviction information, credit history, crime victim status, physical or mental disability, genetic information (including testing and characteristics), sexual orientation, veteran status, uniformed servicemember status, or any other status protected by federal, state, or local laws, that has the purpose or effect of substantially interfering with an individual’s work performance or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive working environment. An individual’s work environment is not limited to the physical location where an individual is assigned to perform work duties.

Again, while it is not possible to list all the circumstances that may constitute other forms of workplace harassment, the following are some examples of conduct that may constitute workplace harassment:
  • The use of disparaging or abusive words or phrases, slurs, negative stereotyping, or threatening, intimidating, or hostile acts that relate to the above protected categories,
  • Written or graphic material that insults, stereotypes, or shows aversion or hostility toward an individual or group because of one of the above protected categories and that is placed on walls, bulletin boards, email, voicemail, or elsewhere on our premises, or circulated in the workplace,
  • A display of symbols, slogans, or items that are associated with hate or intolerance toward any select group.
Persons covered by this policy include full and part-time employees, interns, contractors, vendors, volunteers, or temporary workers hired by the Practice.
Reporting Discrimination and Harassment
If an employee believes that they have witnessed or have been subjected to any form of discrimination or harassment, immediately notify People Operations or any member of executive leadership.

The Practice prohibits retaliation against employees who, based on a reasonable belief, provide information about, complain, or assist in the investigation of any complaint of harassment or discrimination. We will promptly and thoroughly investigate any claim and take appropriate action where we find a claim has merit. To the extent possible, we will retain the confidentiality of those who report suspected or alleged violations of the harassment policy.

Discipline for violation of this policy may include, but is not limited to, reprimand, suspension, demotion, transfer, and discharge. If the Practice determines that harassment or discrimination occurred, corrective action will be taken to effectively end the harassment. As necessary, the Practice may monitor any incident of harassment or discrimination to assure the inappropriate behavior has stopped. In all cases, the Practice will follow up as necessary to ensure that no individual is retaliated against for making a complaint or cooperating with an investigation.
Investigation Procedure
If the Practice receives information that alleges or suggests that an employee has committed an act of sexual harassment or any other form of harassment, the matter shall be referred to People Operations for the purpose of investigating the information or allegation. The Practice shall conduct an immediate investigation of the information or allegation as soon as reasonably possible.

At the conclusion of the investigation, the investigator will report their findings to the designated official. If the investigator concludes that there is significant evidence that the employee has engaged in any form of harassment, as defined in this policy, then that employee shall be subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination. The employee may also be required to participate in counseling, training or other remedial measures.

The People Operations Officer, or other official designated by the Practice will provide written notice to the employee who filed the complaint and the accused employee informing them of the outcome of the investigation. The notification will not include the recommended discipline in cases where a violation of this policy was found.

Every employee shall have a duty to cooperate with any investigation initiated by the Practice. Failure to do so may result in disciplinary action. Additionally, every employee has the duty to be truthful and must disclose all information known to the employee when requested to do so by an appropriate person in the organization or the person designated by the organization to investigate an alleged incident of violence. Any employee who fails to be completely truthful or who withholds information shall be subject to disciplinary action up to and including termination.
Witnessing Sexual Harassment
In addition to having a duty to cooperate with an investigation of sexual harassment, employees who have information about or who witness an act of sexual harassment (or any other form of harassment) against an employee, are required to report all information to People Operations.

The Practice will not retaliate against, terminate, or discipline any employee for reporting information about alleged incidents of sexual harassment (or any other form of harassment), as defined in this policy that may have been committed by any other employee, including a member of management or executive leadership. Any employee who believes they have been subjected to adverse action as a result of making a report pursuant to this policy should contact People Operations.
Statement of Confidentiality
The Practice recognizes and respects an employee’s right to privacy and the need for confidentiality and the freedom to make their own decisions. The Practice shall maintain the confidentiality of an employee’s disclosure regarding sexual harassment to the extent allowed by law, and unless to do so would result in physical harm to any person, and/or jeopardize safety within the workplace. When information must be disclosed to protect the safety of individuals within the workplace, the Practice shall limit what information is disclosed as necessary to protect the safety of the disclosing employee and others, and to comply with the law.

The Practice shall provide advance notice to the employee who disclosed information, to the extent possible, if the disclosure must be shared with other parties in order to maintain safety in the workplace or elsewhere. The Practice shall also provide the employee with the name and title of the person to whom the Practice intends to share the employee’s statements and shall explain why the information must be disclosed.
Leave Requests
The Practice recognizes that victims of sexual harassment may need time off to obtain or attempt to obtain a protection or restraining order or any other legal assistance to help ensure their health, and safety. The Practice will work in collaboration with the employee to provide reasonable and flexible leave options when an employee is a victim of sexual harassment.

The Practice will work with the employee to provide paid leave first before requiring an employee to utilize unpaid leave. An employee must provide reasonable advance notice to the employer of the need to take time off unless advance notice is not feasible. To request leave, an employee should contact People Operations. The Practice will maintain the confidentiality of a person who requests leave under this policy, to the extent allowed by law.

The Practice will also provide reasonable accommodations for a victim of sexual harassment who requests an accommodation for the safety of the victim or to maintain their work performance while at work. Reasonable accommodations may include the implementation of safety measures, including a transfer, reassignment, modified schedule, changed work telephone, changed workstation, installed lock, assistance in documenting the sexual harassment that occurs in the workplace, an implemented safety procedure, another adjustment to a job structure, workplace facility, or work requirement in response to the sexual harassment, or referral to a sexual harassment counseling service. The Practice will assist an employee to enforce his or her protection order, if applicable.
Resolution Outside Company
The purpose of this policy is to establish prompt, thorough, and effective procedures for responding to every complaint and incident so that problems can be identified and remedied internally. However, employees have the right to contact the Illinois Department of Human Rights (IDHR) or the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) about filing a formal complaint. An IDHR complaint must be filed within 180 days of the alleged incident(s) unless it is a continuing offense. A complaint with the EEOC must be filed within three hundred (300) days. In addition, an appeal process is available through the Illinois Human Rights Commission (IHRC), after the IDHR has completed its investigation of the complaint.

3. Practice Employment Policies & Procedures
Employment Authorization Verification
New hires will be required to complete Section 1 of federal Form I-9 on the first day of paid employment and must present acceptable documents authorized by the U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services proving identity and employment authorization no later than the third business day following the start of employment with the Practice. If an employee is authorized to work in this country for a limited period of time, the employee will be required to submit proof of renewed employment eligibility prior to expiration of that period to remain employed by the Practice.
New Hires and Introductory Periods
The first 90 days of a new hire’s employment is considered an introductory and probationary period. During this period, a new employee will become familiar with the Practice, workplace policies and procedures and job duties and responsibilities. The Practice will have the opportunity to monitor the quality and value of an individual’s performance and make any necessary adjustments to the duties and responsibilities of the position. This period introductory and period with the Practice may be shortened or lengthened as deemed appropriate by management and People Operations. Completion of this introductory and probationary period does not imply guaranteed or continued employment. Nothing that occurs during or after this period should be construed to change the nature of the “at-will” employment relationship.
Regular Full-Time Personnel
Regular full-time employees are those who have completed their introductory and probationary period and are regularly scheduled to work a minimum of 32 hours per week. Unless stated otherwise or specifically permitted by law, all the benefits provided to employees are for regular full-time employees only. This includes vacation, holiday pay, health insurance, and other benefits coverage.
Regular Part-Time Personnel
All employees who work fewer than 32 hours per week are considered part time. Part-time employees are not eligible for benefits unless specified otherwise in this handbook, in the benefit plan summaries, or specifically permitted by law.
Training Program
In most cases, and for most departments, onboarding and training new employees is done on an individual basis by the department manager. Even if an employee has had previous experience in the specified functions of the job duties, it is necessary for all new employees to learn our specific processes, procedures, as well as the responsibilities of the specific position. If an employee believes they require additional training, please consult with the Supervisor or Manager.



4. Professional Conduct
Conflicts of Interest
The Practice is concerned with conflicts of interest that create actual or potential job-related concerns, especially in the areas of confidentiality, customer relations, safety, security, and morale. If there is any actual or potential conflict of interest between an employee and a competitor, supplier, distributor, or contractor to the Practice, the must disclose it to People Operations. If an actual or potential conflict of interest is determined to exist, the Practice will take such steps as it deems necessary to reduce or eliminate the conflict.
Criminal Activity/Arrests
The Practice will report all criminal activity in accordance with applicable law. Involvement in criminal activity while employed by the Practice, whether on or off Practice property, will result in disciplinary action including suspension or termination of employment.
Personal Appearance
An employee’s personal appearance reflects on the reputation, integrity, and public image of the Practice. All employees are required to report to work neatly groomed and dressed. Every employee is expected to maintain personal hygiene habits that are generally accepted in the community, including clean clothing, good grooming and personal hygiene, and appropriate attire for the workplace and the work being performed. All staff at the Practice, regardless of position, are required to wear black scrubs. Providers are required to report in the following: (a) business casual attire with a lab coat; or (b) black scrubs. The uniform requirement does not apply to executive leadership.

Fragrant products, including but not limited to perfumes, colognes, and scented body lotions or hair products, should be used in moderation out of concern for others with sensitivities or allergies.

The Practice, in accordance with applicable law, will reasonably accommodate employees with disabilities or religious beliefs that make it difficult for them to comply fully with the personal appearance policy unless doing so would impose an undue hardship on the Practice. Employees should submit an accommodation request in writing to the Chief Wellness Officer or Director, People Operations to begin the interactive process.

Failure to comply with the personal appearance standards may result in being sent home to groom or change clothes. Frequent violations may result in disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment.
Standards of Conduct

Fraternization and Workplace Relationships
The Practice strongly believes that a work environment where employees maintain clear boundaries between an employee’s personal and business interactions is necessary for effective business operations. Although this policy does not prevent the development of friendships or romantic relationships between co-workers, it does establish boundaries as to how relationships are conducted during working hours and within the workplace.

Individuals in supervisory or managerial roles, and those with authority over others’ terms and conditions of employment, are subject to more stringent requirements under this policy due to their status as managers and/or supervisors, their access to sensitive information, and their ability to affect the employment of individuals in subordinate positions.
  • Employees that are involved in a romantic relationship with another employee must disclose the existence of the relationship to leadership (Chief Operating Officer and Director, People Operations).
  • During working hours and while in the workplace, employees involved in a romantic relationship must conduct themselves in an appropriate manner that does not disrupt the workplace or interfere with each individual’s work productivity.
  • During nonworking time, such as lunches, breaks, and before/after work periods, employees engaging in personal exchanges should observe an appropriate workplace manner to avoid offending other employees or putting other employees in an uncomfortable position.
  • During working hours, employees are strictly prohibited from engaging in any physical contact that could in any way be deemed inappropriate by a reasonable person while doing business on behalf of the Practice, whether onsite in the workplace or on client or patient premises.
  • Employees who allow personal relationships with co-workers to adversely affect the work environment will be subject to the Practice’s disciplinary policy, including counseling for minor problems. Failure to change behavior and/or maintain expected work responsibilities and appropriate workplace conduct and professionalism are viewed as a serious disciplinary matters subject to the Practice’s progressive disciplinary policy.
  • Employee off-duty conduct is generally regarded as private, as long as such conduct does not create problems within the workplace. An exception to this principle, however, is romantic or sexual relationships between supervisors and subordinates.
  • Any supervisor, manager, executive or any individual in a leadership role must disclose the existence of a romantic or sexual relationship with another employee who is in a similar role. Disclosure may be made to the Chief Wellness Officer, Chief Operating Officer or the Director of People Operations. Leadership will review the circumstances to determine whether any conflict of interest exists. No supervisor, manager, executive or other individual in a leadership role may engage in any romantic or sexual relationship with a subordinate or direct report even if the relationship is deemed consensual. If an employee in any of the aforementioned roles engages in a romantic or sexual relationship with a subordinate or direct report, such action will be grounds for immediate disciplinary action up to and including termination of employment with the Practice.
  • When a conflict-of-interest or potential risk of conflict is identified due to an employee’s relationship with another employee, the Practice will work with the parties involved to consider options for resolving the issue. The solution may be to ensure the parties no longer work together on matters where one is able to influence the other or take action for the other. Matters such as hiring, firing, promotions, performance management, compensation decisions and financial transactions are examples of situations that will require reallocation of duties to avoid any actual or perceived reward or disadvantage. In some cases, other measures may be necessary, such as the transfer of one or both parties to other positions or departments. If one or both employees refuse to accept the solution offered by the Practice, such refusal will be deemed a voluntary resignation of one or both employees.
  • Failure to cooperate with the Practice to resolve a conflict or problem caused by a romantic or sexual relationship between employees or among managers, supervisors or others in positions of authority may be deemed insubordination and result in disciplinary action up to and including termination.
The provisions of this policy apply regardless of the sexual orientation of the parties involved. Any concerns about the administration of this policy should be addressed to the Director of People Operations. This policy is not meant to preclude or interfere with the rights of employees protected by the National Labor Relations Act or any other applicable statute concerning the employment relationship.
Employment of Relatives or Family Members
The Practice does not prohibit the hiring of related family members. This policy is intended to ensure effective supervision, internal discipline, trust, and seeks to avoid the appearance of, or potential for conflicts of interest, the appearance of impropriety, and conflicts of loyalty associated with nepotism.

“Relative or family member” (also referred to as “related persons”), for purposes of this policy, is defined as a familial or other close relationship, whether by blood, adoption, marriage, or domestic partnership (e.g., a spouse, domestic partner, child, grandparent, grandchild, aunt, uncle, brother, sister, first cousin, niece, nephew, any corresponding in-law or step- or foster relation).

No employee involved in employment decisions may make, participate in, or attempt to influence employment or evaluate decisions involving a relative or closely related person. This policy is intended to prohibit employees who are related from working in direct reporting or supervisory relationships. The policy does not, however, prohibit the Practice from simultaneously employing relatives or closely related persons when the employment relationship does not create a conflict of interest and where the employee has no influence over the terms or conditions of the related person’s employment.

Where employment would be in conflict with the policy, as stated above, the issue may be avoided by an adjustment of the duties assigned to one or both of the individuals or by modification of the duties of their respective position(s) which will ensure that all employment decisions are made by others. A closely related person may be employed in the same facility, department or team only if all employment decisions with respect to the closely related person are made by a manager or member of executive leadership different from the one to whom the person is related. An employee will not be transferred or promoted into a position which involves employment decisions being made concerning the employee by a relative or closely related person. Individuals with departmental responsibilities who may have conflicting relationships under this policy will not have responsibility for reviewing and approving financial, budgetary, or personnel actions initiated by, recommended by, or affecting a relative or closely related person. Familial or other close relationships should be disclosed to executive leadership or People Operations so that they can be evaluated and managed.

If after employment an employee enters into one of the above relationships, one of the affected employees must seek a transfer or a change in the reporting relationship. Such changes must be approved by the Chief Wellness Officer. No exception to this policy will be made without the written consent of the Chief Wellness Officer.

Part-time employees are eligible for holiday pay in proportion to the number of hours they normally are scheduled to work.

Non-exempt employees must work their scheduled work day before and after the holiday period in order to be paid for the holiday unless they are absent with prior permission from their Supervisor or Manager.

An employee may not take an unscheduled PTO or sick day the day before or the day after a paid holiday period. In the absence of mitigating and verifiable circumstances (e.g., family emergency, hospitalization), an employee will not be paid for the holiday.
Paid Time Off (PTO)
The Practice recognizes the need for balance in an employee’s work life and personal life. To that end, the Practice encourages eligible employees to utilize PTO to increase productivity and maintain a healthy morale through rest and relaxation.

Full-time and part-time employees are eligible for paid PTO time. PTO is calculated based on an employee’s anniversary date as follows:
  • After completion of the introductory/probationary period, an employee is entitled to 40 hours of PTO annually during the employee’s first two (2) years of employment.
  • During an employee’s 2nd through 5th years of employment, an employee is entitled to 80 hours of PTO annually.
  • After an employee’s 5th year of employment, an employee is entitled to 120 hours of PTO annually.
In order to preserve continuity and productivity, a department’s coverage must be maintained at all times. PTO requests must be approved in advance by the employee’s Manager or Supervisor to avoid conflicting employee PTO requests. PTO requests will be approved on a first come first served basis. Where a conflict occurs, the efficient operation of the Practice will be the determinate factor in deciding which employee’s PTO request is approved. Part-time employees receive paid PTO time in proportion to the number of hours they are normally scheduled to work.

An employee must submit a PTO request in writing at least four (4) weeks in advance to their direct Supervisor or Manager for approval. PTO may not be carried over from one year to the next nor is PTO pay granted in lieu of taking the actual time off.

Upon termination, eligible employees will be paid for accrued but unused PTO.
Sick Leave
Any employee who works at least 80 hours for the Practice within any 120-day period is eligible for paid sick leave. Employees begin to accrue paid sick leave on the first calendar day after they begin their employment. For every 40 hours worked, employees accrue one hour of paid sick leave. The Practice provides eligible employees five (5) paid sick days per year. New employees may use paid sick leave 90 calendar days after the start of employment. Sick leave must be used in a minimum increment of four (4) hours which equals a ½ day.

Sick leave may be used when:
  • The employee is physically or mentally ill or injured, or for the purpose of receiving medical care, treatment, diagnosis, or preventative medical care;
  • A family member is ill or injured, or for the purpose of receiving medical care, treatment, diagnosis, or preventative medical care;
  • The employee or a family member is the victim of domestic violence; or
  • The employee’s place of business is closed by order of a public official due to a public health emergency, or the employee needs to care for a child whose school or place of care has been closed by order of a public official due to a public health emergency.
For purposes of this policy, “family member” means child, spouse or domestic partner, parent, spouse or domestic partner's parent, sibling, grandparent, grandchild, or any other individual related by blood or whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship. Adoptive, “step,” foster, and legal guardianship relationships are all included within this definition.

An employee’s use of sick leave will not be conditioned upon searching for or finding a replacement worker.

An employee may not take a sick day the day before or the day after a paid holiday period. In order for an employee to be eligible for holiday pay, the employee is required to work the day before and the day after the specific holiday period if the employee is scheduled. In the absence of mitigating and verifiable circumstances (e.g., doctor’s note, hospital admission), an employee will not be paid for the holiday.

If an employee is absent from work for more than three (3) consecutive days, the employee must provide documentation signed by a licensed health care provider OR a police report, court document, signed statement from an attorney, or any other evidence that supports the reason for the absence. When the use of sick leave is reasonably foreseeable (e.g., pre-scheduled health care appointments or court dates in a domestic violence case), the employee is required to provide up to five (5) days’ notice before leave is taken to their immediate supervisor. When the use of sick leave is not reasonably foreseeable, the employee is required to provide notice to their immediate Supervisor or Manager as soon as is practicable on the day he/she intends to take earned sick leave.

Employees may carry over up to twenty (20) hours of unused sick leave to the following year (for a total of sixty (60) hours), except an employee on FMLA may carry over up to forty (40) hours to be used exclusively for FMLA purposes with a maximum usage of sixty (60) hours per year. Unused earned sick leave will not be paid at separation or termination of employment. When employees use sick leave while on FMLA, the notice and documentation and certification requirements under the FMLA, and any other applicable provisions of the FMLA, take precedence to the extent they conflict with a provision of this policy.

The Practice prohibits retaliation against employees for requesting or using earned sick leave. Employees who believe that their legal rights have been violated are encouraged to contact People Operations.
Bereavement Leave
The death of a family member is a time when employees wish to be with their families. Full-time and part-time employees are eligible immediately upon hire for three (3) paid days of bereavement leave for the death of an immediate family member. Members of the immediate family include spouses, parents, brothers, sisters, children, grandchildren, grandparents, parents-in-law and domestic partners and their parents. Part-time employees are eligible for bereavement pay in proportion to the number of hours they normally are scheduled to work.

Effective January 1, 2023, eligible employees may use up to two (2) weeks of unpaid bereavement leave to:
  • Attend the funeral (or an alternative to a funeral) of a covered family member:
    • Child or stepchild
    Spouse or domestic partner
    Sibling
    Parent, stepparent, mother-in-law, or father-in-law; or grandchild or grandparent
  • Plan arrangements necessitated by the death of a covered family member;
  • Grieve the death of a covered family member; or
  • Be absent from work because of a miscarriage and other unsuccessful pregnancy and/or adoption and fertility outcomes
Employee eligibility for this benefit mirrors the FMLA requirements as this is an extension of the FMLA. The leave must be completed within sixty (60) days of the employee receiving notice of the death of a covered family member or within sixty (60) days of the date of the covered event. If an employee experiences the death of more than one family member in a 12-month period, the employee is entitled to up to a total of six weeks of bereavement leave during the 12-month period. Requests for bereavement leave should be made to the People Operations as soon as possible.

Note: Illinois state law does not create a right for unpaid leave that exceeds the unpaid leave allowed under the federal Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA). Therefore, if an employee exhausts all of their FMLA leave during the applicable 12-month period, the Practice is not required to provide additional bereavement leave.
Leave for Domestic, Sexual or Gender Violence Leave
In accordance with the Illinois Victims' Economic Security and Safety Act, employees who are the victims of domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence or who have family or household members who are the victims of domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence whose interests are not adverse to the employee as it relates to the domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence, may be eligible for up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave within any 12- month period, and upon return will be restored to the same or an equivalent position.

Employees may elect to substitute any or all annual or vacation leave, personal leave and sick leave during the otherwise unpaid leave. This substitution of paid leave does not extend the total allowed leave period but runs concurrently with it. Leave under this policy also runs concurrently with Family and Medical Leave when the reason for the leave qualifies for Family and Medical Leave, such as for a serious health condition. In these situations, the leave does not extend any unpaid time available to the employee under Family and Medical Leave.

Reasons for Leave
Eligible employees may take leave under this policy so that they or a member of their family or household may take part in one or more of the following actions:
  • seek medical attention for or recover from physical or psychological injuries caused by domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence;
  • obtain services from a victim's services organization;
  • obtain psychological or other counseling;
  • participate in safety planning, including temporary or permanent relocation, or other actions to increase their physical safety or economic security; or
  • seek legal assistance or remedies to ensure their health and safety, including preparing for or participating in any civil or criminal legal proceeding related to or derived from domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence.
For purposes of this policy, "family or household member" means a spouse or party to a civil union, parent, grandparent, child, grandchild, sibling, or any other person related by blood or by present or prior marriage or civil union, other person who shares a relationship through a child, or any other individual whose close association with the employee is the equivalent of a family relationship as determined by the employee and persons jointly residing in the same household.

Eligible employees must provide the Practice with at least 48 hours advance notice of the need for leave unless such notice is not practicable.

Certification of the Need for Leave
To request leave, the employee must supply the Practice with a sworn statement from the employee that the employee or a family or household member is a victim of domestic violence, sexual violence, gender violence or any other crime of violence and that leave is necessary for one of the reasons described above.

The employee seeking leave also must provide supporting documentation from one of the following sources if the employee has possession of such document:
  • a victim's services organization;
  • a member of the clergy;
  • an attorney;
  • a medical or other professional from which the employee or family or household member has sought assistance;
  • a police report or court record; or
  • any other corroborating evidence.
All information and documentation an employee provides in connection with a request for leave under this policy, including the fact that the employee requested and/or obtained leave, will be held in the strictest confidence — except to the extent the employee requests or consents to any disclosure in writing, or as otherwise required by law. Further, the Practice specifically prohibits any discrimination, harassment, or retaliation against employees who request or take leave under this policy in good faith.

During leave, an employee must provide periodic reports (at least every 30 days) about their status and any change in the employee’s plans to return to work.

During an approved domestic violence leave, the Practice will maintain an employee’s health benefits as if the employee continues to be actively employed. If an employee chooses not to return to work at the end of the leave period, the employee must reimburse the Practice for the cost of any health benefit premiums paid to maintain the health coverage during the leave, unless an employee cannot return to work because of continuation, reoccurrence, or onset of domestic or sexual violence or other circumstances beyond the employee’s control.

Family military leave must be taken during the time federal or state deployment orders are in effect. To be eligible, the employee must have been employed for at least twelve (12) months and have worked at least 1,250 hours during the 12-month period immediately preceding the request for family military leave. Employees may take family military leave only if they have exhausted all accrued vacation, personal, compensatory, and other leave, except sick and disability leave.

The request for leave must be made at least fourteen (14) days in advance if the leave will consist of five (5) or more consecutive workdays. Employees must make a reasonable effort to notify the Practice that they may be absent from or late to work.
Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA)
It is the policy of the Practice comply with the Family Medical Leave Act (FMLA). FMLA provides certain employees with up to twelve (12) weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year. It also requires that an employee’s group health benefits be maintained during the leave. FMLA is designed to help employees balance their work and family responsibilities by allowing them to take reasonable unpaid leave for certain family and medical reasons. It also seeks to accommodate the legitimate interests of employers and promote equal employment opportunity for men and women.

Employees are considered eligible for leave if they have worked for the Practice at least twelve (12) months, with at least 1,250 hours over the past twelve (12) months. All requests for leaves of absence should be submitted in writing to the Director, People Operations.

FMLA provides eligible employees up to twelve (12) weeks of unpaid leave each year for any of the following reasons:
  • for the birth and care of the newborn child of an employee
  • for placement with the employee of a child for adoption or foster care
  • to care for an immediate family member (spouse, child, or parent) with a serious health condition or
  • to take medical leave when the employee is unable to work because of a serious health condition because of any qualifying exigency arising out of the fact that an employee's spouse, son, daughter or parent is a covered military member on covered active duty or called to covered active-duty status (or has been notified of an impending call or order to covered active duty) in the Reserves component of the Armed Forces in support of contingency operations or Regular Armed Forces for deployment to a foreign country. This leave is also available for family members of active-duty service members.
FMLA leave usually will be taken for a period of consecutive days, weeks, or months. However, employees are entitled to take FMLA leave intermittently or on a reduced leave schedule when medically necessary due to a serious health condition of the employee or covered family member or the serious injury or illness of a covered servicemember.

The taking of another job while on family/medical leave or any other authorized leave of absence is grounds for immediate termination, to the extent permitted by law.

During FMLA leave, eligible employees are entitled to receive group health plan coverage (if applicable) on the same terms and conditions as if they had continued to work. Employees may still be required to pay the employee portion of the group health insurance premium.

Employees requesting FMLA leave are entitled to receive written notice from the Practice telling them whether they are eligible for FMLA leave and, if not eligible, the reasons why they are not eligible. When eligible for FMLA leave, employees are entitled to receive written notice of:
  • their rights and responsibilities in connection with such leave;
  • the Practice's designation of leave as FMLA-qualifying or non-qualifying, and if not FMLA-qualifying, the reasons why; and
  • the amount of leave, if known, that will be counted against the employee's leave entitlement.
Employees must provide 30 days' advance notice of the need to take FMLA leave when the need is foreseeable. When 30 days' notice is not possible, or the approximate timing of the need for leave is not foreseeable, employees must provide the Practice written notice of the need for leave as soon as practicable under the facts and circumstances of the particular case. Employees, who fail to give 30 days' notice for foreseeable leave without a reasonable excuse for the delay, or otherwise fail to satisfy FMLA notice obligations, may have FMLA leave delayed or denied.

When employees take intermittent or reduced work schedule leave for foreseeable planned medical treatment for the employee or a family member, including during a period of recovery from a serious health condition or to care for a covered servicemember, the Practice may temporarily transfer employees, during the period that the intermittent or reduced leave schedules is required, to an alternative position with equivalent pay and benefits for which the employee is qualified and which may better accommodate recurring periods of leave.

In addition to the basic FMLA leave entitlement outlined above, an eligible employee who is the spouse, son, daughter, parent or next of kin of a “covered servicemember” is entitled to take up 26 weeks of leave during a single 12-month period to care for the servicemember with a serious injury or illness. Leave to care for a servicemember shall only be available during a single-12 month period and, when combined with other FMLAqualifying leave, may not exceed 26 weeks during the single 12-month period. The single 12-month period begins on the first day an eligible employee takes leave to care for the injured servicemember.

A "covered servicemember" is a current member of the Armed Forces, including a member of the National Guard or Reserves, who is undergoing medical treatment, recuperation or therapy, is otherwise in outpatient status or is on the temporary retired list, for a serious injury or illness. These individuals are referred to in this policy as “current members of the Armed Forces.”

Covered servicemembers also include a veteran who is discharged or released from military services under condition other than dishonorable at any time during the five (5) years preceding the date the eligible employee takes FMLA leave to care for the covered veteran, and who is undergoing medical treatment, recuperation or therapy for a serious injury or illness. These individuals are referred to in this policy as “covered veterans.”

If an employee is approved for FMLA, the employee may be responsible for paying the employee portion of all health insurance premiums. If an employee fails pay to the monthly premium payment in a timely manner, the Practice will provide the employee with information about their rights under COBRA and/or applicable state continuation coverage policies. An employee who fails to make the employee portion of health insurance premiums may be terminated from the group plan.
Personal Leave of Absence
The Practice recognizes that an employee may need time off from work due to special circumstances that other leave policies may not address. In such cases, an employee may request an unpaid personal leave of absence. All regular/full-time/part-time employees employed for at least one (1) year are eligible to apply for a 30-day unpaid personal leave of absence. Job performance, absenteeism, and department/business needs will be taken into consideration before a request is approved. Requests for unpaid personal leave may be denied or granted for any reason and are within the sole discretion of the Practice.

An employee will be required to use all available paid time off (PTO) prior to taking an unpaid personal leave of absence. Sick leave, PTO, seniority, or other benefits will not accrue during an unpaid personal leave of absence. Holidays that occur during an unpaid personal leave of absence will not be paid.

If an employee is granted a personal leave of absence, reinstatement to the employee’s current position or any position is not guaranteed.

The Practice’s employer-sponsored group health benefits will be continued at the same level and under the same conditions as prior to the leave, for up to thirty (30) days. An employee is responsible for payment of the employee portion of the insurance premium while on personal leave.

If an employee is on a personal leave of absence that exceeds thirty (30) days or the employee fails to pay the premium payment in a timely manner, the Practice will provide the employee with information about their rights under COBRA and/or applicable state continuation coverage policies.

An employee is required to return from unpaid personal leave on the originally scheduled return date. Leave extensions will be considered on a case-by-case basis. If the Practice denies the extension request, an employee must return to work on the originally scheduled return date or the employee will be considered to have voluntarily resigned their employment with the Practice.

Return to Work

8. Attendance & Punctuality
Attendance Policy
Attendance and punctuality are key factors in an employee’s job performance. Punctuality and regular attendance are expected of all employees. Excessive unexcused absences, tardiness or leaving early is unacceptable and strictly prohibited.

When scheduled, an employee is expected to report to work on time at the beginning of their scheduled shift, ready to work. The inability to report to work on time as scheduled may lead to disciplinary action, up to and including termination of employment, for violation of the Practice’s attendance policy or job abandonment.

If an employee knows ahead of time that they will be absent or late, the employee must provide reasonable advance notice to their Supervisor or Manager. An employee may be required to provide documentation of any medical reason or other excuse for being absent or late for their scheduled shift.

The Practice reserves the right to apply unused PTO or sick leave to unauthorized absences. Absences resulting from approved leave are exceptions to this policy. Employees are expected to remain at work for their entire shift as scheduled. Late arrival, early departure or other absences from scheduled hours are disruptive and must be avoided.

This policy does not apply to absences covered by the Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) or leave provided as a reasonable accommodation under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). These exceptions are described in separate policies.

Absences
“Absence” is defined as the failure of an employee to report for work when he or she is scheduled to work. The two types of absences are defined below:

An excused absence occurs when all the following conditions are met:
  • The employee provides to their Supervisor or Manager sufficient notice at least 48 hours in advance of the absence.
  • The absence request is approved in advance by the employee’s Supervisor or Manager.
  • The employee has sufficient accrued paid time off (PTO) to cover the absence.
An unexcused absence occurs when any of the above conditions are not met. If it is necessary for an employee to be absent or late for work because of an illness or an emergency, the employee must notify his or her Supervisor or Manager no later than one (1) hour before the employee’s scheduled starting time on that same day. If an employee is unable to call, the employee must have another individual notify their Supervisor or Manager.

When reporting an absence, an employee should indicate the nature of the problem causing the absence and their expected return to work date. A physician’s statement is required for any illness related absence in excess of three (3) consecutive work days. In the event of an emergency, an employee must notify their Supervisor or Manager as soon as possible reasonably practicable.

An unexcused absence is a violation of the Practice’s attendance policy and will result in a verbal warning for the first occurrence. Employees with three (3) or more consecutive days of excused absences because of illness or injury must give the Practice proof of physician’s care and a fitness for duty release prior to returning to work. While proof of physician’s care and/or a fitness for duty release may explain the employee’s absence, it does not excuse the absence and an occurrence may still occur.

If available, an employees must use earned PTO or sick leave for every absence according to Practice policy.

Excessive absences, tardiness or leaving early are grounds for disciplinary action, which may include a verbal warning, written warning, suspension without pay, and/or termination. Depending on the circumstances, including the employee’s length of employment, the Practice may counsel an employee prior to termination for excessive absences, tardiness or leaving early, in accordance with the Practice’s corrective disciplinary procedures.

Attendance During New Hire Orientation Period
A new employee, within their probationary period, who has two (2) or more absences will receive a written warning. If a new employee, within their probationary period, has more than two (2) absences, their probationary period may be extended or their employment with the Practice may be terminated.

Occurrences
An occurrence is documented as any unapproved absence, tardy, leave early, or missed time clock in/out that is not approved in advance by an employee’s Supervisor or Manager.

Tardiness and Early Departures
Employees are required to return from scheduled breaks on time. Employees, when scheduled, are required to work to the end of their scheduled shift. Employees who must leave work before the end of their scheduled shift must notify their Supervisor or Manager immediately. An employee who violates this policy is subject to disciplinary action which may include a written warning, suspension without pay, or termination of employment.

An employee is deemed to be tardy when:
  • The employee fails to report for work at the assigned/scheduled work time; or
  • The employee takes an extended meal or break period without prior approval from their Supervisor or Manager.
An employee is deemed to have an early departure when:
  • The employee leaves work prior to the end of assigned/scheduled work time without prior approval from their Supervisor or Manager.
Time Clocks and Failure to Clock In/Out
Employees are required to follow established guidelines for recording their actual hours worked. A missed clock in/out is a violation of this policy and includes:
  • Failure to clock in/out at the beginning and/or end of their assigned shift;
  • Failure to clock in/out on their designated time clock for the meal break;
  • Failure to accurately report time worked; or
  • Clocking in/out early (or late) of assigned shift without prior approval
Departmental Notification Procedure
Employees are expected to follow departmental notification procedures if they will be late for work, will not be at work, or are requesting planned time away from work. Employees must submit time off requests in advance to their Supervisor or Manager in accordance with departmental procedures.

At the time of notification, the employee must notify their Supervisor or Manager when an absence is due to a documented/approved leave of absence (e.g., Military Leave, FMLA) in order to ensure appropriate tracking of leave utilization and absenteeism. Incidents of not following the departmental notification procedures, including No-Call/No-Show, will be addressed in accordance with the Progressive Discipline policy.
Departmental Policy Approval Process
Practice departments may develop a departmental attendance/punctuality policy that meets the unique operational needs of the department. If executive leadership determines that a departmental policy is needed, the department will follow its approval process to establish the policy which requires final approval from executive leadership. This policy is intended as a guideline to assist in the consistent application of policies and programs for employees. The policy does not create a contract implied or expressed, with any Practice employees, who are employees at will. The Practice reserves the right to modify this policy in whole or in part, at any time, at the discretion of the Practice.
Disciplinary Action
Excessive absenteeism is defined as two (2) or more occurrences of unexcused absences within a 30-day period and will result in disciplinary action. Excessive tardiness or absenteeism is subject to disciplinary action up to and including suspension without pay and/or termination of employment.

Employees who are absent without notification (No Call/No Show) to their Supervisor or Manager are subject to immediate disciplinary action which may include suspension without pay or immediate termination of employment with the Practice.

Supervisors and Managers are expected to monitor their direct reports’ attendance on a regular basis and address unsatisfactory attendance in a timely and consistent manner. If the Supervisor or Manager notices a pattern of unscheduled usage of accrued time off banks, they should discuss the concern with the employee.

When an employee has been previously counseled under the Progressive Discipline Policy the totality of the circumstances will be assessed when determining further disciplinary action. For situations involving suspension or termination of employment, the Supervisor or Manager should consult with People Operations prior to implementing disciplinary action.

Timely and regular attendance is a part of job performance and it is the expectation that all Practice employees must follow established attendance policies. Consequently, employees who have exhibited unsatisfactory attendance which results in disciplinary action (whether verbal or written) may have the behavior documented during their annual performance evaluation.
Holidays
If an employee calls out of work the day before or the day after a paid Practice holiday, the employee if eligible will not receive holiday pay. The employee may also be subject to progressive disciplinary action.



9. Performance Management
Progressive Disciplinary Process
Violation of Practice policies or procedures may result in disciplinary action including demotion, transfer, leave without pay, or termination of employment. The Practice encourages a system of progressive discipline depending on the type of prohibited conduct. However, the Practice is not required to engage in progressive discipline and may discipline or terminate employees who violate employment policies and procedures. An attempt at progressive disciplinary action does not imply that employment is anything other than on an “at-will” basis.

In appropriate circumstances, management will first provide an employee with a verbal warning, then proceed to the next step in the progressive disciplinary process. If an employee’s behavior is not sufficiently altered, eventual demotion, transfer, forced leave, or termination of employment will occur.
  • Coaching is the first step in the progressive disciplinary process and is a useful tactic that may help deal with challenges before they become issues. Coaching is a conversation with an employee wherein the Supervisor or Manager speaks with the employee about a performance gap or expectations not being met relating to the employee’s job duties. Coaching may be documented.
  • Verbal Warnings are used to address relatively minor performance deficiencies or issues that may lead to additional corrective action if an employee fails to take decisive action and correct the behavior.
  • Written Warnings are intended to address more serious and/or frequent deficiencies and make the employee aware that continued employment is at risk if immediate and lasting improvement does not occur.
  • Final Warnings are issued if an employee’s performance does not improve following a Written Warning or if the performance deficiencies are more severe than those appropriate for a Verbal or Written Warning. In certain circumstances, a Final Warning may be given to address a serious performance deficiency wherein continued recurrence of the behavior will result in termination of employment.
Supervisors and Managers will make every effort possible to allow an employee to respond to any disciplinary action taken. While the Practice will attempt to be consistent in the enforcement of its policies, the Practice is not obligated to follow any disciplinary or grievance procedure and depending on the circumstances, an employee may be disciplined or terminated without any prior warning or procedure.

Behavior that is illegal is not subject to corrective discipline, and such behavior may be reported to local law enforcement authorities. Similarly, theft, substance abuse, intoxication, fighting and other acts of violence in the workplace or at a Practice-sponsored event are also not subject to corrective discipline and may be grounds for immediate termination.
Performance Evaluations
The Practice will review each employee’s work performance. At the end of an employee’s initial 90-day introductory/probationary period, the employee’s direct Supervisor or Manager will evaluate an employee’s performance for continued employment.

Regular full-time and part-time employees will receive an annual performance evaluation. The performance evaluation process is a means for increasing the quality of an employee’s performance and to provide feedback in needed areas of improvement. An employee’s initiative, effort, attitude, job knowledge, and other factors will be addressed. An employee must understand that a positive performance review does not guarantee a pay raise or continued employment. Pay raises and promotions are based on many factors, including job performance.
Bonus Compensation
The Practice provides bonus compensation to eligible employees. To be eligible for the bonus payment, an employee:
  • Must have been employed as of July 1st of the bonus plan year.
  • Must have a minimum of “meets expectations” in all performance categories of the employee’s annual performance evaluation.
  • Must be employed with the Practice as of December 31st of the bonus plan year.
  • Must be employed with the Practice at the time bonus payments are made to eligible employees.
Each eligible employee’s bonus is calculated as a percentage of base salary received as of year-end. Bonus payments will range from 3%-8% of an employee’s base salary. An employee’s performance, tenure, and overall contribution to the success and profitability of the Practice are utilized in determining an employee’s bonus payment. Employee bonus payments are authorized at the sole discretion of the Chief Wellness Officer.

Note, employee bonus compensation is based on year-end profits of the Practice. If the Practice is not profitable in a given year, no bonus payments will be made to any employee.



10. Workplace Policies & Procedures

The Practice maintains separate medical records files and personnel files for all employees. Supervisors, Managers and others in management may have access to personnel files for employment-related decisions. If an employee wishes to review their personnel or medical records file, an employee may submit the request in writing to the Director, People Operations. After submitting the written request, an employee may review their personnel and/or medical documents within ten (10) business days. Any inspection must occur in the presence of a Practice representative.

All requests by an outside party for information contained in an employee’s personnel file, and/or for copies, must be directed to the People Operations Department, which is the only department authorized to provide such information.
Bulletin Boards
The Practice maintains an official bulletin board in Practice facilities to provide employees with official Practice notices, including wage and hour laws, changes in policies, and other employment-related notices. At times the Practice may also post information of general interest to employees on the bulletin board. Each employee is responsible for being informed about this material by periodically reviewing the bulletin board. Only authorized personnel may add and remove notices from the bulletin board.
Inclement Weather/Emergencies
The Practice recognizes that inclement weather and other emergencies may affect an employee’s ability to get to work. In such situations, an employee’s safety is paramount. If an employee is unable to safely travel to work for their scheduled shift, the employee must notify their Supervisor or Manager. If the employee has PTO available, the employee may be charged PTO to cover the absence.

Company Closure
Examples of emergencies when the Practice may close include, but are not limited to, closures for power outage, extreme temperature or weather conditions.

Notification
In an emergency, the Practice will make every effort to notify an employee of a facility’s closure. These notification efforts may be transmitted via text, phone call or email. When the Practice is unable to notify employees of an office closure, an employee should use common sense to assess the safety and practicality of the situation. In a regional power outage, for example, the Practice is unlikely to have power. If there is reported flash flooding in an employee’s area, the employee should report to work only if the employee is able to commute safely.

Partial-Day Closure
If an emergency event such as inclement weather or a power outage occurs, the Practice may decide to close midday. When the Practice closes mid-day, employees will be instructed to leave immediately so that the conditions do not further deteriorate and affect employees’ ability to travel safely.

If an exempt employee is at the office or is working from home, with permission, the employee will be paid their normal salary for the week. If a nonexempt employee is at work on a day the Practice closes early, the employee will be paid for hours worked.

Notification of Closure Prior to Reporting to Work
Nonexempt employees that are notified of a closure prior to reporting to work will not be paid during the closure. An exempt employee will be paid their normal salary for the week.

Extending Leave
When the Practice closure ends, employees are expected to report to work. An employee must contact their Supervisor or Manager if the employee is unable return to work at the end of the closure. The Practice recognizes that an employee may need additional time off to repair extensive home damage or for other emergency situations. Unique circumstances may affect an employee’s ability to report to work even when the Practice is able to remain open. Each employee’s situation will be assessed on a case-by-case basis.

The Practice recognizes that in a severe national or regional disaster, all methods of communication may be unavailable; however, an employee must attempt to contact their Supervisor or Manager, by any method possible.

If an employee is unable to work in a situation where the Practice remains open, the employee is required to use any available PTO or if the employee has no PTO remaining, the time off will be unpaid.

12. Safety & Security
Drug and Alcohol Policy
The Practice is committed to providing a safe, healthy, and productive work environment. Consistent with this commitment, it is the intent of the Practice to maintain a drug and alcohol-free workplace. Being under the influence of alcohol, illegal drugs (as classified under federal, state, or local laws), or other substances which may impair an employee’s ability to perform their job duties in the workplace during working hours may pose a serious health and safety risk to others, and will not be tolerated.

Prohibited Conduct
The Practice expressly prohibits employees from engaging in the following activities when they are on duty or conducting Practice business or on Practice premises (whether or not they are working):
  • The use, abuse, or being under the influence of alcohol, illegal drugs, or other impairing substances.
  • The possession, sale, purchase, transfer, or transit of any illegal or unauthorized drug, including prescription medication that is not prescribed to the individual, or drug-related paraphernalia.
  • The illegal use or abuse of prescription drugs.
While the use of marijuana has been legalized under some state laws for medicinal and/or recreational uses, it remains an illegal drug under federal law. The Practice does not discriminate against employees solely on the basis of their lawful off-duty use of marijuana. An employee may not consume or be under the influence of marijuana while on duty or at work. If an employee has a valid prescription for medical marijuana, the employee should refer to the Practice Disability Accommodation policy for additional information.

Nothing in this policy is meant to prohibit appropriate use of over-the-counter medication or other medication that may legally be prescribed under both federal and state law if it does not impair an employee’s job performance or safety or the safety of others. If an employee takes over-the-counter medication or other medication that is legally prescribed under both federal and state law to treat a disability, the employee must inform their Supervisor or Manager if the medication may impair an employee’s job performance, safety, or the safety of others. If an employee believes they need a reasonable accommodation before reporting to work while under the influence of that medication, the employee should refer to the Practice Disability Accommodation policy for additional information.
Drug-Free Workplace
The Practice, in compliance with the federal Drug-Free Workplace Act of 1988, has adopted the following policy that must be adhered to as a condition of employment:
  • The unlawful use, possession, manufacture, dispensation, or distribution of controlled substances in all work locations is strictly prohibited.
  • Any employee convicted of a criminal drug statute violation occurring in the workplace must notify their Supervisor, Manager or the Director, People Operations of the conviction within five (5) days after the conviction. As required by the federal Drug-Free Workplace Act of 1988, the Practice must inform contracting or granting agencies of such convictions within ten (10) days after receiving notification of a conviction.
  • Upon receiving such notification, the Practice, in conjunction with the location concerned, will take all steps necessary to assure the proper conduct of sponsored projects and programs. If a decision is reached to allow the affected individual to continue employment with the Practice, the individual must participate in and satisfactorily complete an approved drug abuse assistance or rehabilitation program.
The Practice will evaluate its obligations in accordance with state and other applicable laws where required, on a case-by-case basis.
Random Drug Testing
All employees are subject to the Practice’s random drug-testing policy to ensure employees are in compliance with the drug-free workplace policy. Employees will be tested for their use of commonly abused controlled substances, which include amphetamines, barbiturates, benzodiazepines, opiates, cocaine, methadone, methaqualone, phencyclidine (PCP), propoxyphene and chemical derivatives of these substances.

Employees who refuse to submit to a test or who adulterate, dilute, or otherwise tamper with a test specimen are subject to immediate termination. Employees must advise the testing lab of all prescription drugs taken in the previous month before the test and must be prepared to show proof of such prescriptions upon request.

If an employee tests positive on an initial screening test, the employee will be temporarily suspended without pay while the confirmation test is being conducted.

A positive test result confirmed by a medical review officer of the laboratory will result in disciplinary action, up to and including termination. Discipline selected by the Practice will depend on a variety of factors, including the prior work record of the employee, the length of employment, the prior accident and attendance record of the employee, the circumstances that led to the testing, and proposals by the employee to address the problem.

Employees will be required to submit to random drug testing and/or whenever the Practice has a reasonable suspicion that an employee has violated any of the rules set forth in this policy. Reasonable suspicion may arise from, among other factors, supervisory observation, co-worker reports or complaints, performance decline, attendance or behavioral changes, results of drug searches or other detection methods, or involvement in a workrelated injury or accident.

The Practice may provide reasonable accommodations to employees who misuse alcohol or drugs by providing unpaid leave to enroll in a qualified treatment program if the employee discloses the need for treatment prior to the employee receiving a positive drug test for a controlled substance. Confidentiality of records and information will be maintained in accordance with all local, state, and federal laws.

Entrance into a treatment program does not relieve an employee of the obligation to satisfy Practice's standards regarding employee performance, and participation will not prevent the Practice from administering discipline for violation of its policies or relieve the employee of his or her responsibility to perform his or her job in a safe and efficient manner.

Employees may use accrued, unused sick leave when taking leave under this policy. Failure to enter, remain or successfully complete a qualified treatment program may result in termination of employment.

The Practice may require any employee who has gone through substance abuse treatment to be evaluated by a Practice-selected Provider before being allowed to return to work. If an employee has any questions about this policy, the employee should contact the People Operations Department.

  • Physically injuring another person.
